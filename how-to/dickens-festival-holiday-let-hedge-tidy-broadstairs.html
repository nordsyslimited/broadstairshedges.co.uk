Kerb-appeal hedge work for Broadstairs holiday-let owners.

Broadstairs has a serious holiday-let stock. The 2021 census counted 1,907 holiday homes in Thanet and the number rose about 10% through 2022. In CT10 specifically, the sold-price average sits at £403,990 over the last twelve months (Rightmove), which skews above the district average and matches an Airbnb segment paying real money for a garden that photographs well. Timing the hedge work around the three fixed points in the town's calendar — Dickens Festival, August bank holiday and school-holiday changeovers — is a specific job with specific rules.

The three fixed points in the Broadstairs calendar

Broadstairs Dickens Festival — annually every June, running since 1937, 89th edition in 2026. Third week of June. The town's holiday-let stock books out for the whole week. Dickens House Museum on Victoria Parade runs its programme; there are marquee events in Pierremont Park and the Bandstand at Viking Bay. Guests pay a premium; owners have a clear before-and-after in guest reviews if the front hedge looks tired.

August bank holiday weekend — the town's single biggest tourism weekend. Viking Bay hits capacity. Holiday-let owners want the changeover cleaning contractors and the front-of-house tidy done in the same 5-hour Saturday window between guest checkout and check-in. A hedge trim booked for that Saturday morning is standard, and it needs to be a same-day in-and-out.

School holiday changeovers — Fridays and Saturdays through July and August. The Airbnb price model rewards visibly-tidy hedges at listing photograph time and again at the guest arrival moment. What that means for hedge work: the tidy has to be booked for the day before the changeover Saturday, not the Saturday morning itself (too much competing traffic on the road, contractor vans stack up on Stone Road and Ramsgate Road).

The nesting-law complication

The awkward bit. The three peak dates — Dickens Festival in June, August bank holiday, July school holidays — all fall inside the March-August nesting window. The Wildlife & Countryside Act 1981 makes it an offence to disturb an active nest, and on the Broadstairs coast the additional herring-gull red-list overlay applies to any hedge within gull range. So a hedge that hasn't been touched since March cannot be freshly hard-cut in June for kerb appeal.

The workaround that keeps you legal

Book a late-May pre-season cut before nesting proper is peaking. Then light top-and-face tidies in June and August on the surveyed-clear hedge — no hard reductions, no base work, no removal, no disturbance of any active nest. The hedge stays sharp through the peak weeks; the law stays satisfied. This is what I do for the holiday-let stock I look after; it is the only pattern that actually holds up.

What the tidy actually looks like

A holiday-let front-hedge tidy is not the same job as an annual maintenance cut. It is 1 to 2 hours, one operator, small-scale kit, and specifically:

  • Face-clip on the street-facing side, sharp defined lines. Top-clip level.
  • Retrieve fallen debris around the base — clifftop wind chucks a surprising amount of general litter into the base of Escallonia and Griselinia hedges.
  • Sweep the pavement.
  • Leave the rear-facing side and interior alone unless there is a clear cosmetic issue from inside the property.
  • Arisings bagged and taken away same day. On the Airbnb turnover schedule, leaving a garden waste bag on the pavement between checkout and check-in defeats the point.

Not a lawn cut, not a border tidy, not a full annual. The point is high-quality kerb appeal for the guest arrival, and that is a targeted job with a specific spec.

Access and scheduling on a changeover Saturday

Practical realities for Airbnb owners:

  • Get in before the cleaner arrives. Book the hedge for Friday afternoon or Saturday 08:00-09:30. The cleaner blocks the driveway and interior access from about 10:00.
  • Key access. Owners with a smart-lock code or a key safe make this a much cleaner operation. Meeting on-site to hand over a key on a peak Saturday morning is a false economy.
  • Adjacent parking. Broadstairs' town-centre streets are mostly permit or pay-and-display, and the peak-day loading options are constrained. I know which streets I can pull up on for a 90-minute window without a ticket; most owners don't.
  • Waste route. TDC's £73/year garden waste subscription accepts stems under 7.5cm — fortnightly all year, so a holiday-let fed with regular light trims fits within it. Heavier arisings go to Margate HWRC on Manston Road (free for Kent residents, booking required, Monday-Saturday 08:00-16:30, Sunday 09:00-16:00). Note: no separate Manston HWRC exists — Margate is the site.

Between-let contracts vs one-offs

Two patterns work for holiday-let owners in Broadstairs; a third does not.

The seasonal contract. A late-May cut, three light tidies through June-August timed to specific guest turnovers, and a firm late-September cut to close the season. Fixed price for the year, priority booking through the peak weeks. This is what most of the owner-operator stock in Stone Bay and Viking Bay uses.

The on-demand tidy. Message on the WhatsApp two or three days before a specific high-value guest arrival. Slot in if the diary allows, no guarantee in Dickens Festival week or bank-holiday week. Works for smaller portfolios or owners who don't run the property year-round.

The "cheap annual". A single hard September cut and nothing else. This is the pattern that consistently underperforms. By June the hedge has put on 40cm of new coastal growth, looks ragged in the listing photographs, and the guest reviews mention it. On a £403k-average CT10 plot letting for £160+/night in July, the extra £180 for a seasonal contract pays back in one three-star-avoided review.
