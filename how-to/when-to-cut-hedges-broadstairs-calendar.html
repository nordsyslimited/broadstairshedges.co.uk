When to cut hedges in Broadstairs — a month-by-month calendar for CT10.

Broadstairs runs on a specific calendar. Nesting law kicks in early March. The Dickens Festival lands the third week of June and empties the town's holiday-let stock into paying guests. August bank holiday is the biggest single weekend of the year on Viking Bay. The salt-air evergreens want two cuts a year, not one. Here is what a working contractor's diary looks like, month by month, on the Thanet coast.

January — quiet, useful, deciduous only

The quietest month, and by design. This is renovation-cut territory on deciduous hedges: hawthorn, blackthorn, inland beech, mixed native field hedges on the Reading Street fringe. Hard reshaping — back to the framework, three-year rebuild plans — belongs here. Evergreens are dormant enough that touching Escallonia or Griselinia now risks cold-damage on the cut faces if a hard easterly comes through. Leave them.

February — the pre-nesting surge

The last ten days of February are the busiest week I book all year outside September. Nesting law starts biting on 1 March in practice (the Wildlife & Countryside Act 1981 doesn't put a hard calendar date on it, but active nests are almost universally in place from early March onward). Owners with hawthorn, holly, laurel or mixed native hedges book this week because it is the last legal window for hard work before the eight-month nesting shutdown. Book it in December if you want it — by mid-February the window is full.

March to August — the nesting window

Section 1 of the Wildlife & Countryside Act 1981 makes it an offence to intentionally damage or destroy an active nest, or to disturb a nesting bird protected by Schedule 1. Penalties: up to six months in prison and an unlimited fine per nest. On the Broadstairs coast the additional wrinkle is the herring gull — moved to the UK Red List in 2021 — which now needs an individual Natural England licence to disturb. General licences have been withdrawn.

What can actually happen March-August?

Formative cuts on young hedges (year 1-3 plantings) where nesting is unlikely. Light top-trims on evergreens where a visual check confirms no active nests. No hard reductions, no removal, no coppicing, no base work, and nothing at all on cliff-edge hedges within herring-gull range without a licence. The bar for "check first, cut second" is genuine, not decorative.

June — Dickens Festival week

The Broadstairs Dickens Festival runs every June and hits its 89th edition in 2026. Third week of the month, Dickens House Museum on Victoria Parade as the anchor, marquee events at Pierremont Park. The town's holiday-let stock is booked solid the week before; owners want kerb-appeal tidy-ups. The problem: this collides with peak nesting. What I offer that week is light front-of-house top-trims on hedges I've already surveyed clean earlier in the season — not fresh jobs on hedges I haven't seen.

July — the pause

Almost nothing on the coast in July. Salt-scorch is at its worst, growth flush is still finishing, and any evergreen taken at height in July looks browned along the cuts by the following weekend. Inland CT10 sites (St Peter's outskirts, Reading Street) tolerate a July trim on privet and Elaeagnus but I'd rather do it in late May or wait till September.

August — bank-holiday kerb appeal

Bank-holiday weekend is the town's biggest single tourism weekend. Airbnb owners want tidy-ups the week before; the same nesting-window caveats as June apply. Late August is also the classic yew cut window — single annual clip, holds sharp definition through winter. Holly and laurel take a late-August cut well too. Hawthorn stays off-limits until November.

September — the big window

The single most productive month on the Thanet coast. The late-summer growth flush has finished. Owners want the hedge tidy for the last of the holiday-let season and before winter sets in. Coastal evergreens — Escallonia, Griselinia, Olearia, Euonymus, Elaeagnus — get their second cut of the year (the first is late May). This is when the two-cut cadence pays: one light cut in late May, one firmer cut in mid-September, and the hedge stays dense and defined all year. Book September in June if you can; the diary fills fast.

October — the calm-weather reduction window

Best month for hard reductions on the clifftop. Growing season is over, gales have not yet arrived in force, birds have dispersed. Leylandii staged reductions, holm oak reshapes, escallonia hard cuts — all belong here. If you have been putting off a big job, October is the answer.

November — deciduous work begins

Leaf drop opens the deciduous window. Hawthorn, blackthorn, mixed native and inland beech all become workable again. First storms of the season often come through in early November — Storm Ciarán landed 1-2 November 2023 and did structural damage across CT10 gardens — so windthrow assessments become part of the walk-round.

December — the maintenance-cut month

Structural work on deciduous hedges. Winter renovations. Weather-dependent — Broadstairs sees roughly 612.6mm of rain a year at Manston, most of it split thinly across autumn and winter, so December is generally workable more days than not. Christmas week fills fast for tidy-ups on the holiday-let stock ahead of New Year bookings.

Species timings, in one line each

  • Escallonia, Griselinia, Olearia, Euonymus, Elaeagnus — late May and mid-September. Two light cuts beat one hard one on the coast.
  • Yew — single cut in late August.
  • Holly — late August or early September.
  • Laurel — late summer, or April/May before nesting proper starts.
  • Holm oak — single cut, late August or September.
  • Leylandii, cypress — one annual cut in late August or September; never into brown wood.
  • Hawthorn, mixed native, inland beech — November to February only, nesting law willing.
  • Privet — twice a year, June and September, if inland. Once in September if coastal.
