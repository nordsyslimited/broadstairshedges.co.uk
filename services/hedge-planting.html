Service · Hedge planting

The right species for Canterbury chalk, properly planted.

New hedge planting done with the local soil and climate in mind — not a generic species list. Honest steers away from box (after the blight crisis) and Leylandii (after the 1970s mistake), towards species that actually want to live here.

Species we recommend for Canterbury

For formal low to mid-height hedges (knee to chest height)

  • Yew (Taxus baccata) — the downland classic. Native to the North Downs chalk. Drought-tolerant once established. Will outlast the building. Slow first three years, then steady.
  • Ilex crenata (Japanese holly) — the closest visual replacement for box, blight-resistant. Good on chalk, good on the deeper loam in the Stour valley. Cultivars 'Convexa' or 'Stokes' are the box-substitute standard.
  • Euonymus japonicus — tolerates partial shade and a hard clip; the dark horse for older Canterbury gardens where mature trees have closed the canopy.

For taller boundary hedges (head height up)

  • Hornbeam (Carpinus betulus) — better than beech on Canterbury's thin chalk topsoil, particularly in frost-pocket positions. Keeps its russet leaves through winter.
  • Beech (Fagus sylvatica) — fine on the deeper Stour-valley loam in Wincheap, St Dunstan's, Northgate, but struggles on thin downland topsoil during dry summers.
  • Holly (Ilex aquifolium) — slow, dense, evergreen, native; brilliant for wildlife and birds.
  • Privet (Ligustrum ovalifolium) — fast, cheap, semi-evergreen on chalk in cold winters. Right answer for "I need a screen, now."

For native mixed wildlife hedges (Blean fringe and downland villages)

A 50% hawthorn, 25% blackthorn, mix of field maple, hazel, dog rose, holly, guelder rose. Maps to the native field hedges already in the landscape. Plants in late autumn for spring establishment. Cuts in winter only (nesting law).

What we don't recommend

  • New box — box blight and box tree moth are both established in Canterbury. New box plantings have a realistic 5-10 year window before the same problems return. Read the full guide.
  • Leylandii — fast and cheap, but you will be reducing or removing it within 15 years. The cost saving up front is usually wiped out by the reduction job later.
  • Cherry laurel in small gardens — gets too big too fast for the position; ends up looking thuggish.

Planting practice

Bare-root for the November-to-March window (cheaper, establishes faster, looks bare for the first season). Container-grown for spring or summer planting (more expensive, can plant any month). Either way: dig a trench not a hole, mycorrhizal fungi at planting, water in heavily, mulch deep with bark, and water through the first two summers — Canterbury's chalk is drought-prone and new hedges die from drought far more than from pest pressure.

Pricing

Planted hedges are quoted including plants, planting, mulch and aftercare advice. Bare-root native mix is typically £25 – £40 per metre planted. Container-grown yew or Ilex crenata at usable size: £60 – £150 per metre planted. The big variable is plant size — we quote multiple options so you can balance budget against visible-from-day-one impact.
