Service · Hedge reduction

Bringing overgrown Broadstairs hedges back into proportion.

The job for hedges that have outgrown the position — too tall, too wide, leaning, or shading out the rest of the garden. Different work from a routine cut, with different risks and different timing.

When reduction is the right call

  • The hedge is over the 2m height that triggers the High Hedges (Anti-Social Behaviour Act 2003 Pt 8) route if a neighbour complains — Thanet District Council's formal fee for that complaint is £650
  • It's growing into the upstairs windows of a Victorian four-floor terrace on Gladstone Road or the Stone Bay belt
  • It's shaded out half the garden behind it
  • It's leaning across the path or the neighbour's boundary
  • An older Leylandii screen has hit 5-6m and become an annual liability

How reduction differs from cutting

A routine cut takes off the year's growth. A reduction takes the hedge back into older wood — sometimes by 1m, sometimes by 3m. Different species handle this very differently:

  • Yew, holly, hornbeam, beech, privet, hawthorn — handle hard reductions well. Regenerate from old wood. Best done in late winter (February).
  • Leylandii, thuja, cypress — do not regenerate from brown wood. Cut back past the green needle-bearing zone and that face stays brown forever. Reduction is possible but it is a planning job, not a sawing job. On clifftops there is a separate windthrow question: the shallow root plate on chalk means a big top-heavy Leylandii that is suddenly opened up to a fresh salt-wind vector can fail at the root — the tree does not die from salt, it topples in a February gale.
  • Cherry laurel — handles hard reduction but looks rough for 18 months while it regenerates.
  • Escallonia, Griselinia, Olearia — coastal evergreens regenerate reasonably well from older wood but need staged reductions if you want any early density back. One-shot hard reductions leave a bare face all summer.

I tell you honestly at quote stage how much you can take off and how the hedge will look during the recovery period.

Staged reductions

For a mature Leylandii on a Broadstairs clifftop plot, staged reduction is nearly always the right answer — first cut to an intermediate height, allow new growth to fill the sides, then drop to final height the following winter. Costs more in total than a one-shot reduction, but the hedge survives and the windthrow risk is managed.

Conservation-area work

Reduction work is much more likely to trigger the s.211 notice than routine cutting — once you are cutting back into older wood, you are frequently above the 75mm-at-chest-height threshold. I file the notice with Thanet District Council on your behalf six weeks before the work, included in the quote. Read the CA rules explainer.

Pricing

Reduction jobs are quoted per job, not per hour. Most domestic reductions in Broadstairs fall in the £300 – £900 range depending on length, height, species and access. Big mature Leylandii or laurel reductions on clifftop plots can be more — staged where needed.
