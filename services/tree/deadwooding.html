Tree services · Deadwooding

Taking the dead wood out — safer tree, healthier tree.

Deadwooding is exactly what it sounds like: removing the dead, dying and diseased branches from the canopy of a live tree. It's not structural work. The tree keeps its outline and its live crown. What comes down is the wood that was going to fall on its own — and the disease pathways that were spreading from it.

Why deadwood

  • Falling risk. Dead branches shed on their own; the only question is whether they land on a path, a car, a shed roof, or a person. Big garden sycamores and holm oaks in Broadstairs are the usual culprits — a 4-metre dead limb at 8 metres up is a serious impact if it drops, and Thanet winter storms accelerate the timetable.
  • Disease pathway. Dead wood in the canopy is an entry point for fungi and pathogens back into the live tree. Removing dead limbs promptly closes those pathways.
  • Insurance. Most household insurers expect reasonable arboricultural maintenance of trees overhanging property. Documented deadwooding is what "reasonable" looks like.

What I don't take

I deadwood conservatively. Small dead twigs deep in the interior of the canopy that pose no fall risk are habitat for insects, fungi and hole-nesting birds — and on Broadstairs seafront plots that habitat matters, because the Thanet Coast SSSI/SPA/Ramsar boundary is close. What comes off is the material that has real weight, real height, or real disease propagation — nothing else. If a customer wants an over-aggressive clean-out, I push back; that's not deadwooding, that's aesthetic thinning, and there's a name for the honest version of that: crown thinning.

Frequency

Most mature garden trees in Broadstairs do well with a deadwood on a 2–4 year cycle. Species that shed heavily (sycamore, lime, London plane) at the tighter end; slower species (holm oak, yew, hawthorn) at the wider end. Coastal wind exposure moves the cycle a touch tighter — dead branches on a clifftop tree don't hang on as politely as they do inland.

Special case: ash dieback (Hymenoscyphus fraxineus)

Ash dieback is well-established across east Kent, including in the mature hedgerows and shelter-belts around St Peter's and Reading Street. An ash tree with dieback needs an honest assessment before any work — brittle wood, unpredictable failure points, and no benefit to the tree from repeated deadwooding once dieback is beyond a certain stage. In many advanced cases the correct call is a controlled fell rather than a deadwood. I flag it, I don't hide it.

Before I cut

Deadwooding is exempt from section-211 notice on strict reading — dead wood is not "healthy tree material" — but I still check for TPOs and inform Thanet District Council in the CA notice as a courtesy, and I photograph before-and-after for insurance evidence.
